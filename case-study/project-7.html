SeeThrough — Making the invisible visible through clinically-grounded Mixed Reality simulation

Architects follow building codes without understanding vision impairment. Fixed goggles hand you someone else's diagnosis. SeeThrough built a dual-mode MR/VR platform — grounded in real clinical data and lived community experience — that lets designers feel what they build before it's permanent.

01 — The problem

Accessibility in the built environment has a persistent failure mode: the people designing spaces rarely experience them through the eyes of the people they're designing for. Building codes set a compliance floor, but compliance is not the same as access — and the gap between the two is full of invisible barriers that cost people their independence.

The problem runs deeper than intention. Most design professionals genuinely want to build inclusively. But without the ability to experience vision impairment firsthand during the design process, they default to code minimum — abstract numbers on a page rather than felt, embodied constraints. The result is spaces that pass inspection and still fail the people who use them.

The empathy gap — what architects see vs what vision-impaired users experience

Four compounding problems

Code ≠ Access

Architects and planners follow building codes without understanding the lived experience of vision impairment — compliance becomes the ceiling, not the floor.

Retrofit cost

Uninformed design decisions create permanent physical barriers. Discovered post-construction, these are expensive, disruptive, and sometimes structurally impossible to fix.

Static tools

Traditional simulation tools — fixed-lens goggles, printed overlays — can't adapt to new digital designs and can only represent a single, one-size-fits-all impairment profile.

A fourth systemic problem compounded all of these: a global shortage of accessibility specialists means most projects never receive professional assessment at all. Accessibility evaluation is bottlenecked behind a credential that's always in short supply.

02 — Research & clinical grounding

The first and most critical decision in this project was to resist the temptation to ship fast and approximate. "Blurry filter" simulations exist — and they are actively harmful, because they give designers false confidence that they understand something they have only gestured toward. Clinical accuracy was a non-negotiable foundation.

Medical literature review

I conducted a structured review of clinical literature on the primary vision conditions that affect spatial navigation: Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD), Glaucoma, Cataracts, tunnel vision, and colour vision deficiency across red, green, and blue channels. Each condition has a distinct perceptual signature — and required a distinct simulation approach.

Community partnership — Blind Low Vision NZ

Clinical data describes the mechanics of vision impairment. It does not describe what it is like to live with one, navigate a built environment with one, or be failed by a space that assumed you don't exist. Partnering with Blind Low Vision NZ grounded every simulation decision in authentic daily experience — catching assumptions in the research model that would have produced a tool that felt accurate but wasn't.

Blind Low Vision NZ provided lived-experience validation throughout the simulation design process — ensuring overlays reflected not just clinical descriptions of vision conditions, but the real perceptual challenges they create in spatial environments.

Existing tool audit

I tested and documented seven existing accessibility simulation approaches — from physical lens overlays to video-filter-based apps. All shared the same core limitation: a fixed, non-adjustable representation that collapsed the spectrum of vision impairment into a single default. None could be applied to a digital architectural model before construction. None supported layering of conditions to reflect comorbidity.

Existing tool audit — 7 simulation approaches evaluated, none digitally adaptive

03 — Key insights

"A blurry filter is not vision impairment. It's a designer's idea of what vision impairment might look like. The difference matters — because one produces empathy and the other produces assumption."

— Research principle guiding the clinical accuracy standard
  • Simulation accuracy is an ethical requirement, not a feature. An inaccurate simulation is worse than no simulation — it gives practitioners false confidence that they have understood an experience they have only approximated. Clinical grounding was the project's foundational commitment.
  • Vision impairment is a spectrum, not a state. AMD presents differently at different stages and under different lighting conditions. Glaucoma's peripheral loss has a specific geometric character. Cataracts produce diffuse scatter, not uniform blur. The tool needed to represent this complexity rather than flatten it.
  • The design studio and the construction site are different problems. A designer reviewing a BIM model in their office and a site inspector walking through a half-finished building need different modes. The dual VR/MR architecture wasn't a feature — it was the insight that the tool had to meet professionals where they already were.
  • Compliance is a lagging indicator. By the time a building fails an accessibility inspection, the decisions are already made and the concrete is already poured. The tool's value was in moving evaluation upstream — to the moment decisions are still cheap to change.

04 — Design & development

Dual-mode architecture

The core architectural decision was to build two distinct simulation modes within a single platform, each optimised for a different moment in the design and construction lifecycle.

Mode 01 — MR Auditing
"See-Through" — Physical space with digital overlays

Mixed Reality mode overlays clinically-accurate visual impairment simulations onto a passthrough view of the real physical environment. Professionals walk through existing or under-construction spaces while experiencing the environment as a person with low vision would.

Context: construction sites, existing buildings, post-build audits
Mode 02 — VR Pre-Visualisation
Design-stage simulation before a brick is laid

Virtual Reality mode renders architectural designs in a navigable 3D environment with impairment overlays applied. Designers can walk through a building that doesn't yet exist and identify access failures at the point where changes are still inexpensive.

Context: design studios, architect reviews, client presentations

Clinically-grounded condition mapping

Each simulated condition was built from clinical source material and validated against lived experience. The goal was not visual similarity to a photograph of impaired vision — it was functional equivalence: a simulation that produces the same navigation challenges in a built environment that the real condition produces.

AMD (Macular Degeneration)

Central vision loss with a variable scotoma — modelled with adjustable size and density to reflect disease stage progression.

Glaucoma / Tunnel Vision

Progressive peripheral field loss with a clinically-informed vignette pattern. Severity and gradient adjustable across profiles.

Cataracts

Diffuse forward light scatter rather than uniform blur — capturing the glare sensitivity and contrast loss characteristic of lens opacity.

Central Blind Spot

Static or scotoma-mapped central occlusion — models the navigation strategies required when direct fixation produces no usable vision.

Colour Vision Deficiency

Independent red, green, and blue channel filters — allowing designers to test contrast, wayfinding signage, and hazard marking under each type of colour deficiency.

Layered / Comorbid Profiles

Any combination of conditions can be stacked — e.g. Blur + Partial Blind Spot + Colour Fade — to reflect the real spectrum of lived visual experience.

Condition layering UI — adjustable severity, combined impairment profiles, real-time preview

Workflow presets

A key workflow insight: accessibility professionals need to test the same impairment profile across multiple design iterations, not rebuild conditions from scratch each time. Workflow Presets let users save a named configuration — condition types, severity levels, colour filters — and re-apply it instantly across project stages or client reviews.

Preset Save named impairment profiles (e.g. "AMD Stage 2 + Red-Green CVD") for reuse across project stages
Severity Each condition has adjustable severity parameters — simulate early, mid, and late-stage presentation within one session
Toggle Instant before/after toggle — switch between normal vision and simulated impairment without removing the headset
Mode switch Seamless VR ↔ MR transition — move from reviewing a design model to auditing the physical construction site without reconfiguring

05 — Key UX features

Customisable vision conditions

The simulation moves beyond one-size-fits-all by letting users build layered impairment profiles. A combination like "Blur + Partial Blind Spot + Colour Fade" reflects the lived reality of people who experience multiple conditions simultaneously — something no single-condition goggle could approximate. This is where the tool's clinical grounding becomes a practical UX advantage: designers stop thinking in diagnoses and start thinking in lived experience.

Dual-mode interface — seamless context switching

The VR and MR modes share a consistent interface layer — the same condition controls, the same preset system, the same severity sliders — so there's no learning curve when switching contexts. A designer who uses VR mode to review a model on Monday can walk the building site in MR mode on Wednesday with no additional training.

VR mode — architectural model with Glaucoma overlay, pre-construction review
MR mode — passthrough physical environment with AMD central vision overlay

Interactive visual feedback in real time

Impairment overlays respond dynamically to the user's position, movement, and gaze — not as a static filter on the entire view, but as a spatially-aware simulation that shifts correctly as the user turns their head and moves through the space. This spatial fidelity is what makes the simulation useful rather than merely illustrative: it replicates the navigational challenge of vision impairment, not just its appearance.

Real-time overlay system — spatially-aware, gaze-responsive, head-tracked simulation

What this project represents

SeeThrough sits at an intersection that most portfolios don't reach: clinical research, spatial computing, accessibility advocacy, and software development — unified by a single UX problem. The "Empathy Gap" in architectural accessibility isn't solved by better rules. It's solved by giving the people who make decisions the ability to feel the consequences of those decisions before they're locked in concrete.

This project is the clearest demonstration in my portfolio of research-led product development: a problem identified through structured investigation, a solution validated against community expertise, and a technical implementation that serves the research question rather than the other way around.
