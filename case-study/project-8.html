Doctoral Research — HCI / PhD

Social Buddy — Designing an AI companion to reconnect medically isolated adolescents through shared play

Medical isolation severs the peer connections teenagers depend on developmentally — but returning to social interaction after illness is its own anxiety. This PhD research designed, built, and rigorously evaluated an AI-powered NPC "social buddy" inside a collaborative VR game, and turned the findings into generalisable design guidelines for socially facilitative AI.

01 — The problem

Adolescents undergoing extended medical treatment face a compounding problem: illness isolates them physically from their peers at exactly the developmental stage where peer relationships are most critical. When they eventually return — or try to maintain connection remotely — they carry new weight: self-stigma about their illness, anxiety about being perceived as "different," and a loss of the easy, unconscious social fluency that comes from daily peer contact.

Standard digital communication tools — video calls, messaging apps — don't help. They compress the most anxiety-inducing format of social interaction (direct face-to-face conversation) into a small, unavoidable frame. For a teenager already anxious about how they're perceived, that format amplifies the pressure rather than removing it.

"How do we design an intervention that removes the pressure of direct face-to-face conversation, bypasses the awkwardness, and allows vulnerable teens to comfortably interact with their friends?"

— Research question driving the entire doctoral programme
Social anxiety

Self-stigma transforms ordinary digital communication into a source of stress, not relief, for medically isolated adolescents

Tool mismatch

Zoom and texting demand exactly the kind of direct, scrutinised interaction that generates the most anxiety — they solve the wrong problem

Design gap

No evidence-based guidelines existed for designing AI that facilitates — rather than replaces — human peer connection in vulnerable populations

02 — Research approach

Researching with vulnerable adolescents requires methodological care that goes beyond standard UX practice. Direct access to patients in active medical treatment is ethically constrained and clinically impractical. I designed a four-phase research programme that built knowledge incrementally — using proxies safely, involving participants meaningfully, and validating rigorously.

Phase 01 Generative Discovery

Conducted surveys and semi-structured interviews with healthcare professionals as expert proxies — safely defining user needs and interaction boundaries without burdening vulnerable patients. The goal was to establish the psychosocial landscape before any design work began.

Phase 02 Participatory Co-Design

Facilitated a five-session co-design study with teenagers — collaboratively conceptualising the AI companion's persona, mapping desired user agency, and defining the core game mechanics. Participants were treated as genuine design authorities, not test subjects.

Phase 03 Iterative Prototyping

Translated co-design outputs into the Social Buddy VR prototype across three iterations — built in Unity 3D with OpenAI for natural language and Microsoft Azure Cognitive Services for speech. Each iteration refined the AI's behaviour, spatial navigation, speech characteristics, and interruption handling through formative usability testing.

Phase 04 Evaluative Research

Spearheaded a rigorously controlled mixed-methods evaluation with 20 participants across 10 sessions (remote and in-person). Synthesised qualitative thematic analysis with advanced quantitative statistical modelling — specifically Linear Mixed Effect Models — to measure the AI companion's causal impact on social dynamics during play.

03 — The Social Buddy prototype

The Social Buddy is an AI-powered non-player character embedded inside a collaborative multiplayer VR game. Rather than adding a chatbot or a social skills tutorial, the design decision was to place the AI inside a shared activity — so conversation became a side effect of play, not the point. The shared game context removed the direct scrutiny of face-to-face interaction while creating natural reasons for collaboration, communication, and mutual support.

AI architecture

The companion's conversational intelligence used OpenAI's language model for natural dialogue generation, constrained by a carefully designed prompt framework developed from the Phase 1 clinical research. Azure Cognitive Services handled speech-to-text and text-to-speech — allowing the NPC to speak and respond in real time rather than through text menus.

A key design constraint, derived from co-design sessions: the AI should never try to solve the social problem directly. Its role was to create moments — prompts, activities, gentle redirects — that made peer-to-peer interaction the natural outcome.

04 — Key findings

The evaluative study produced both qualitative evidence of changed social dynamics and quantitative confirmation via statistical modelling. The findings were unexpected in their nuance — the AI companion worked, but not always in the ways anticipated. Several failure modes were as instructive as the successes.

"Quantitative data proved that inclusion of the CVC resulted in a statistically significant decrease in players' Sense of Being Alone — yielding a large effect size — particularly for players less familiar with VR environments."

— Evaluative study finding, Phase 4
  • Proactive AI assistance worked — within limits. The companion's proactive interventions significantly improved participants' perceived Competence and reduced in-game boredom (Negative Affect). But the window for effective intervention was narrow.
  • Timing is everything. Poorly timed interruptions — even well-intentioned ones — disrupted organic peer collaboration and eroded trust in the AI's social intelligence. The system needed to know when to stay quiet as much as when to speak.
  • Imperfection was an asset. Players did not require a perfectly humanoid companion. Humorous quirks — confused navigation, unexpected verbal reactions — became shared sources of amusement. The AI's limitations created shared moments rather than detracting from the experience.
  • The Social Attractor risk. A novel and important finding: if the AI was too engaging, it drew excessive attention away from the human peer. One player could become so focused on the AI that their partner experienced a "Sense of Being Ignored" — the opposite of the intervention's goal.

05 — Evidence-based design guidelines

The ultimate research output was not the Social Buddy prototype — it was the generalised, evidence-grounded UX design guidelines for teams building socially facilitative AI. These are derived directly from the evaluative study findings, not from general intuition.

G-01 Design AI as a social catalyst, not a social replacement

The AI companion's goal is to ease social isolation by enriching human interaction — not by substituting for it. Every AI prompt and behaviour should be oriented toward creating conditions for peer-to-peer connection, not toward becoming the primary social object itself.

Guideline

Use indirect, team-based framing for AI prompts (e.g. "Could you support my teammate?") rather than direct commands. Indirect framing proved highly effective in prompting players to offer peer-to-peer assistance without feeling directed by the AI.

G-02 Embrace humorous imperfection as an icebreaker mechanism

Players consistently used the AI companion's quirks and unexpected behaviours as shared reference points — laughing together at the AI's confused navigation or unexpected verbal reactions. These moments of shared amusement were among the most powerful social facilitators observed in the study.

Guideline

Design the AI to react playfully and allow for occasional quirky or unexpected behaviour. Scripted imperfection — a confused movement, an unexpected remark — becomes a shared source of amusement, actively reducing social awkwardness between unfamiliar players.

G-03 Balance proactivity with strict interruption management

Proactive AI assistance significantly improved perceived Competence and reduced boredom — but the same proactivity, poorly timed, disrupted organic peer collaboration and damaged trust. The system must distinguish between moments of genuine need and moments of natural peer flow that should be left alone.

Guideline

Implement context-aware interruption handling with a model that detects active peer collaboration states. Provide flexible inclusion controls so the group can adapt the AI's presence level in real time — ensuring it enhances rather than derails natural interaction flow.

G-04 Actively mitigate the "Social Attractor" risk

A genuinely engaging AI companion creates a unique UX tension: it can become so interesting that one player's focus shifts entirely to the AI, leaving their human peer with a "Sense of Being Ignored." This is a novel failure mode with no precedent in non-AI facilitation design.

Guideline

Actively programme the AI to redirect attention back to human players. When the AI explicitly prompts one player to assist another, it successfully shifts focus back to the human partner — mitigating neglect and re-centering the peer relationship.

G-05 Use AI presence to directly reduce the sense of being alone

The quantitative evaluation confirmed that the AI companion produced a statistically significant, large-effect-size reduction in participants' reported Sense of Being Alone — with the strongest effects for players who were less familiar with VR and therefore more likely to feel isolated within the environment.

Guideline

For applications serving users who may be unfamiliar with virtual environments, prioritise AI companion presence early in the session. The initial environment-acclimation period is when isolation risk peaks and AI social scaffolding has the largest measurable impact.

06 — Outcomes & significance

The Social Buddy research produced three distinct outputs: a validated VR prototype, a set of five evidence-based design guidelines for socially facilitative AI, and a rigorous mixed-methods evaluation methodology applicable to future HCI research with vulnerable populations.

Why this project matters to my practice

Social Buddy is the deepest piece of research in this portfolio. It required me to synthesise clinical knowledge, participatory design ethics, AI system design, VR interaction design, and rigorous quantitative methods within a single doctoral programme. Every UX decision was answerable to an evidence standard — not just to taste or precedent.

The output isn't just a prototype. It's a transferable knowledge contribution: five guidelines that any product team building AI social tools can use, derived from real participants experiencing real social challenges. That is the difference between UX research as a portfolio artefact and UX research as a practice.
